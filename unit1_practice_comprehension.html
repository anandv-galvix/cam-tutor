🦚 The Proud Peacock and the Sparrow
An original fable for practice

One bright morning, a magnificent peacock spread his feathers in the palace courtyard, dazzling all who passed. His tail shimmered like a jewelled fan, rippling with a hundred eyes of blue and green. No creature, he was quite certain, could match his splendour.

A small brown sparrow hopped nearby, her feathers plain and unremarkable.

"Look at me!" declared the peacock, fanning his spectacular tail still wider. "No creature in this garden is as magnificent as I am. What can a dull little bird like you possibly offer the world?"

The sparrow said nothing. She simply opened her wings and rose swiftly into the clear blue sky, soaring above the trees, above the rooftops, above the very clouds. The peacock watched her go. He spread his own glorious wings — but though they were beautiful, they were far too heavy to lift him beyond the ground.

As summer ended and the days grew short, the sparrow flew south to warmer lands, singing her cheerful song. The peacock remained in the cold courtyard, his magnificent tail dragging in the mud.

"How foolish I have been," he shivered, "to value beauty above everything else."

The moral: True worth lies not in appearance, but in what you can do.

Section B — Comprehension (8 marks)
Q32 marks
Why was the peacock unable to fly, even though he had wings? Use evidence from the text in your answer.
Find the exact quote from the text that explains why. It is in paragraph 4.
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
Although the peacock had wings, they "were far too heavy to lift him beyond the ground." This suggests that his wings were designed for beauty and display rather than for actual flying. The very thing that made him appear magnificent was the thing that prevented him from being truly capable.
📝 1 mark for evidence from text. 1 mark for explanation of what this shows.
Q42 marks
How does the writer use language to show the peacock is proud and boastful? Find ONE example and explain its effect.
Look at what the peacock says and how he says it. What verb does the writer use instead of "said"? What does the peacock literally ask the sparrow?
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
The writer uses the declarative verb "declared" — "declared the peacock" — which suggests a loud, confident public announcement, showing he wanted everyone to hear how wonderful he is. Furthermore, his question "What can a dull little bird like you possibly offer the world?" is actually a rhetorical insult — he is not seeking an answer, he is dismissing the sparrow to make himself seem superior.
📝 1 mark for identifying language feature + quote. 1 mark for explanation of effect.
Q52 marks
"His tail shimmered like a jewelled fan." What technique is used here? Explain its effect on the reader.
Does it use "like" or "as"? What type of figurative language uses these words? What picture does "jewelled fan" create?
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
This is a simile — the peacock's tail is compared to "a jewelled fan" using the word "like." The effect is to create a vivid, glamorous image: "jewelled" suggests sparkling colours and preciousness, while "fan" describes the peacock's tail shape perfectly. Together, they make the peacock seem dazzlingly beautiful — but the comparison to a decorative accessory also hints at his vanity, since a fan is ornamental, not useful.
📝 1 mark for identifying simile. 1 mark for explaining the effect with reference to specific words.
Q62 marks
How does the writer contrast the sparrow and the peacock? Give TWO examples with explanations.
Think about: (1) How they look — peacock's tail vs sparrow's feathers. (2) What they can DO — compare what happens when they each try to use their wings.
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
First, the writer contrasts their appearance: the peacock's tail "shimmered like a jewelled fan" while the sparrow's feathers are described as "plain and unremarkable." This immediately shows one is visually spectacular and the other is ordinary. Second, the writer contrasts their abilities: the sparrow "rose swiftly into the clear blue sky, soaring above the clouds", whereas the peacock's wings were "too heavy to lift him beyond the ground." The contrast shows that beauty can be a disadvantage, while plainness can hide great ability.
Section C — Grammar (4 marks)
Q72 marks
Find TWO modal verbs in the passage. For each one, copy the sentence it appears in and explain what the modal verb shows.
Modal verbs: can, could, may, might, should, would, will, must, ought to. Look through the passage for any of these words.
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
"No creature...could match his splendour" — "could" shows ability/possibility; the peacock believes absolutely that nothing is capable of matching him. "They were far too heavy to lift him beyond the ground" — the implied modal is "could not": the peacock cannot fly. This shows physical impossibility — a permanent limitation. (Also acceptable: "might roar" implied, "can" in the moral context.)
Q82 marks
Write down TWO possessive adjectives or possessive pronouns from the passage. For each one, explain what it refers to.
Possessive adjectives: my, your, his, her, its, our, their (come BEFORE a noun). Possessive pronouns: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs (stand ALONE). Look through the passage for examples.
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
"his tail" — possessive adjective; "his" refers to the peacock; it shows that the tail belongs to the peacock. "her wings" / "her feathers" — possessive adjective; "her" refers to the sparrow; it shows the wings/feathers belong to the sparrow. (Any two correct examples with correct explanation = 2 marks.)
Section D — Extended Response (11 marks)
Q95 marks
How does the writer contrast the personalities of the peacock and the sparrow? How does each character's personality lead to a different outcome? Use evidence from the text and the PEE method. Write in full paragraphs.
Structure: Para 1 — peacock's personality + evidence + explain. Para 2 — sparrow's personality + evidence + explain. Para 3 — how their personalities lead to different outcomes. Contrast words: "in contrast", "unlike", "whereas", "however".
✅ Model Answer (5 marks)
The peacock is portrayed as vain and arrogant. He "declared" to the courtyard that "no creature is as magnificent as I am", using a confident, public verb that shows he wants an audience for his boasting. This vanity is ultimately his downfall: he remains in the cold courtyard as winter comes, his "magnificent tail dragging in the mud" — the very thing he was proudest of now humiliates him.

In contrast, the sparrow is quietly confident and capable. When insulted, she "said nothing" — she responded not with words but with action, "rising swiftly into the clear blue sky." This shows she had no need to defend herself because she knew her own worth. While the peacock is trapped by his beautiful but useless wings, the sparrow thrives and survives the winter.

The contrast shows that the peacock valued appearance over ability, while the sparrow valued action over words. Their different personalities directly cause their different outcomes: the peacock's vanity leads to cold and humiliation, while the sparrow's quiet competence leads to freedom and survival.
📝 Marks: 1 — peacock personality + evidence. 1 — sparrow personality + evidence. 1 — explanation of contrast. 1 — link to outcomes. 1 — quality of language and paragraph structure.
Q106 marks
Is "The Proud Peacock and the Sparrow" a fable? Explain your answer using at least FOUR features of a fable. Give evidence from the text for each feature. Write in full paragraphs.
Fable features: short story, animal characters, animals act like humans (anthropomorphism), a clear problem, a moral/lesson, characters represent human types (stereotypes), teaches a universal lesson. Identify 4 of these and find evidence for each.
✅ Model Answer (6 marks)
"The Proud Peacock and the Sparrow" is clearly a fable because it contains multiple key features of the genre. First, it uses animal characters that behave like humans — this is called anthropomorphism. The peacock "declared" an opinion and felt shame at the end; neither is possible for a real bird. Second, the story is short and simple, with only two characters and one central lesson — typical of fables, which need clarity to teach effectively.

Third, the characters represent human stereotypes: the peacock represents vanity and pride (the strutting, show-off type), while the sparrow represents quiet competence and humility. These types are recognisable because they reflect real human personalities. Fourth, and most importantly, the story ends with a clear moral: "True worth lies not in appearance, but in what you can do." This stated lesson is the defining feature of a fable — every event in the story exists to teach this one truth.

In conclusion, this story fulfils all the defining features of a fable: animal characters with human qualities, a simple structure, recognisable character types, and an explicit moral. It is a modern fable in the tradition of Aesop.
📝 Marks: 1 mark per feature correctly identified and supported with evidence (max 4). 1 mark for overall argument/conclusion. 1 mark for quality of writing.
