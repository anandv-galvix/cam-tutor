🐾 What is Anthropomorphism?
This is the big word used to describe when animals are given human qualities in stories!

📌 Breaking down the word

Anthropo (Greek) = human / person
Morph (Greek) = shape / form
Together: giving human shape or qualities to non-human things (animals, objects, nature).

In fables, animals act like humans — they speak, they think, they feel emotions, and they make decisions. This is anthropomorphism.

🌟 Why use anthropomorphism?

By giving animals human qualities, fable writers can:
• Teach lessons about human behaviour without criticising a specific person
• Make the story easier to understand for young children
• Use animal stereotypes (ideas we already have about animals) to quickly establish character
• Make the moral memorable through a simple, vivid story

💬 Think about this...

When you imagine an ant, what words come to mind? When you imagine a grasshopper? Aesop chose these animals carefully because we already THINK of ants as busy and grasshoppers as carefree. These pre-existing ideas are called stereotypes. Do you think this was a fair choice?

🎬 Famous examples of anthropomorphism in films

You've seen this technique in many famous stories:
• The Lion King — lions feel love, grief, and responsibility
• Charlotte's Web — a spider writes words and forms deep friendships
• Kung Fu Panda — animals practise martial arts and have pride and self-doubt

📌 Why do stories have stages?

A story structure helps the reader follow and understand the narrative. In fables, the structure is very clear because the lesson needs to come through strongly. Learning to identify these stages will help you in your comprehension exams!

🌟 Story Stage Language

Use these words when writing about story structure:
Exposition: "At the beginning of the story... / The story opens with..."
Problem: "The central issue arises when... / The conflict begins..."
Rising action: "As the story develops... / The tension builds when..."
Climax: "The turning point comes when... / At the most dramatic moment..."
Resolution: "By the end of the story... / The lesson is revealed when..."

📌 Animal Stereotypes

A stereotype is a general, fixed idea about a type of person or thing. In fables, animal stereotypes are used to quickly establish characters. We expect ants to be busy and industrious, and grasshoppers to be carefree — so Aesop built his characters around these expectations.

✏️ Write a Character Paragraph
Use the PEE method and the writing frame to describe Ant's OR Grasshopper's personality!
🏆 The PEE Paragraph Structure
P

POINT — What is your main idea?

State clearly what you want to say about the character. Be direct!

"Ant is an extremely hard-working and responsible character."
E

EVIDENCE — What proof do you have?

Quote or refer specifically to something from the text.

"This is shown by the verb 'toiling', as the writer says Ant was 'toiling just a little harder'."
E

EXPLAIN — Why does this prove your point?

Connect your evidence back to your point. Explain what the word/phrase shows.

"The word 'toiling' suggests exhausting, demanding work, showing Ant pushed herself beyond what was comfortable to prepare for winter."

📖 Model Paragraph — Ant

Ant is an extremely hard-working and prudent character. This is shown through the verbs the writer uses to describe her movement — she "bustled", "scurried" and was "toiling just a little harder", all of which suggest non-stop, energetic, purposeful work. Furthermore, the writer shows Ant's intelligence through her words: "Summer won't last forever, you know!" The modal verb "won't" shows Ant's certainty about the future, revealing that she thinks clearly and plans ahead. Overall, Ant is a sensible, responsible character whose behaviour teaches us the importance of preparing for difficult times.

🧠 Comprehension Questions
Use everything you've learned in this lesson to answer these exam-style questions.
Question 1 2 marks
What is "anthropomorphism"? Give one example of anthropomorphism from "The Ant and the Grasshopper".
💡 Exam Tip: Define the term clearly (1 mark), then give a specific example from the story (1 mark).
Anthropo = human, morph = form/shape. Think about what the animals do in the fable that real animals cannot do. Speak? Think? Feel emotions?
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
Anthropomorphism is the technique of giving human qualities, emotions, and behaviours to animals or non-human things. An example from the story is when the Ant "snapped" at Grasshopper and "rolled her eyes" — these are human gestures of frustration and disapproval that a real ant would never show.
Question 2 3 marks
Explain the structure of "The Ant and the Grasshopper". Identify THREE stages of the story and explain what happens at each stage.
💡 Use story stage language: "At the beginning...", "The problem develops when...", "The resolution comes when..."
Stage 1: Setting + characters (summer meadow, Ant working, Grasshopper playing). Stage 2: The problem/issue (Ant warns Grasshopper; he ignores her). Stage 3: The resolution (winter comes, Grasshopper suffers, lesson is learned).
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
At the beginning of the story, the setting and characters are introduced. It is summer in a meadow, and we see Ant working hard while Grasshopper sings and plays. The problem develops when Grasshopper teases Ant for working so hard, and Ant warns him that "summer won't last forever" — but he ignores her. The resolution comes when winter arrives: Grasshopper is cold and starving, while Ant has stored enough food. Grasshopper finally understands the lesson: "How foolish I have been!" This three-part structure — a situation, a problem, and a resolution — is typical of fables.
Question 3 5 marks
Compare the personalities of Ant and Grasshopper. How does each character behave and what does this show about them? Use evidence from the text and the PEE method in your answer. Write a full paragraph of at least 6 sentences.
💡 Extended writing: This is worth 5 marks, so aim for 5 separate, developed points. Compare the TWO characters directly. Use: "In contrast to Ant, Grasshopper..."
Structure: (1) Point about Ant + evidence + explain. (2) Point about Grasshopper + evidence + explain. (3) Contrast — use "In contrast..." or "Unlike Ant...". (4) How do their personalities lead to different outcomes? (5) What do their personalities teach the reader?
✅ Model Answer (5 marks)
Ant and Grasshopper have very different personalities, which leads to very different outcomes. Ant is portrayed as diligent and far-sighted: she "scurries back and forth" collecting food all summer, never stopping to "admire the glorious day". The verb "scurry" suggests purposeful, energetic movement with clear goals, showing she is entirely focused on the future. In contrast, Grasshopper is carefree and irresponsible. He spends summer "chirruping and singing without a care in the world", suggesting he lives only for the present moment. When he "guffawed" at the idea of working in summer, his loud mocking laughter shows he thinks Ant is foolish — he completely misjudges the situation. These contrasting personalities create the central conflict: Ant's wisdom and responsibility are rewarded, while Grasshopper's laziness and short-sightedness are punished. The writer uses these two characters to show readers that preparing for the future, even when times are good, is a sign of wisdom rather than weakness.
📝 Mark scheme: 1 mark each for: identifying Ant's quality with evidence and explanation; identifying Grasshopper's quality with evidence and explanation; direct contrast between the two; connecting personalities to outcomes; overall insight about the fable's message.
