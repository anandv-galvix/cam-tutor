📖 Auntie Anthea and Gentle Geoffrey

A modern retelling of the Ant and Grasshopper — with a twist! Hover/click the underlined words to learn their meanings.

Before you read...

This story is a modern retelling — it takes the SAME characters and basic idea as The Ant and the Grasshopper, but changes the setting, language, and ending. Look out for:
✦ How the characters are different from the original
✦ The moment when the story takes a surprising twist!
✦ Funny moments the author has added

Summer had been a blast and Gentle Geoffrey had loved every minute. He felt so inspired and his music was sweeter than ever. Auntie Anthea had also enjoyed the summer — Geoffrey's music had certainly made cleaning and collecting food seem much less effort.

As the weather cooled, Geoffrey's chirrupingchirruping: a cheerful, musical sound — like chirping crickets! became a little less cheerful. Auntie Anthea, on the other hand, was still scrubbing and storing.

By the time the winter chill set in, Geoffrey felt famishedfamished: extremely hungry — so hungry you feel weak! and frail. He'd played music all summer with his head in the clouds, so he had no home and no food. But just as he thought he should do something, he caught a faint melody in the whistling wind and once more could think of little else.

Auntie Anthea, meanwhile, was warm and well-fed but was finding winter dull. All of a sudden, she thought of Geoffrey.

'What a tasty treat!' she clapped. 'I adore insects and Geoffrey will make a delicious difference!'

'A...A...Auntie Anthea,' shivered Geoffrey, surprised to see her braving the frosty fields. 'Wh...wh...what are you doing here?'

'I couldn't help thinking of you turning into a block of ice out here,' said Anthea, trying to keep the anticipation in her voice at bay. 'I want a tenanttenant: someone who pays to live in someone else's home or space., and who could be more delicious, I mean delightful, than you?'

'D...d...delicious? That doesn't sound so c...c...cool,' quaveredquavered: spoke in a shaky, trembling voice — usually because of fear or cold! Geoffrey.

'Well, it's not — my heart's set on taking you home for supper,' luredlured: persuaded someone to come somewhere by offering something tempting. Anthea. 'Just come on inside for a bit.'

'A b...b...bit of what?'

'A bit of a bite, of course!' snapped Anthea, with a touch of irritation.

Geoffrey, revived by the warmth of Anthea's hearth, smiled and picked up his instrument.

'Auntie, the other insects said you were mean, but you seem so kind. What can I do to thank you for your hospitality?'

And Geoffrey played and sang so beautifully that Anthea sat down, forgot all about her supper plans, and smiled for the first time all winter. They struck a deal: Geoffrey sang for his supper — rather than becoming it — and Anthea had company through the long cold months.

The moral: One good turn deserves another.

🤔 Quick Check: What was Anthea's REAL plan when she invited Geoffrey inside?
Anthea's real plan was to eat Geoffrey for supper! She kept using words like "delicious" and "a bit of a bite" — she wanted him as food, not just as a tenant. The twist is that Geoffrey's beautiful music changed her mind, and they ended up helping each other instead.

📊 Comparing the Two Versions

How is the modern version similar to and different from the original?

Why do writers retell traditional stories?

Writers retell classic fables and tales to make them feel fresh, funny, or to give them a new moral. They often change the setting, add humour, or give characters a surprise twist to make readers think in a new way!

💭 Your Opinion

Which version do you find more interesting and why? Give TWO reasons.

Choose ONE version. Give two clear reasons. Use the comparison table to help — think about: Which moral do you prefer? Which ending feels more satisfying? Which style of writing do you enjoy more?
📋 Model Answer
I find the modern version more interesting because, firstly, it is funnier — the idea that Anthea wants to eat Geoffrey creates dark humour and dramatic irony that the original lacks. Secondly, the ending feels more satisfying because BOTH characters benefit from the deal they strike, which makes the moral feel more positive and hopeful. The original's ending, where Grasshopper simply suffers with no resolution, feels harsh by comparison.
Accept any well-reasoned answer supporting either version, as long as TWO clear reasons are given.

✍️ Summary Writing

A summary gives the MAIN points in a short, clear way.

What is a Summary?

A summary is a short version of a longer text. You include only the MOST IMPORTANT information — not every detail. A good summary:
✦ Gives the main events in order
✦ Does NOT include minor details
✦ Uses your own words where possible
✦ Is much shorter than the original

Sentence Frame — use these to help you

Auntie Anthea and Gentle Geoffrey is a modern retelling of...
The story is set in...
The two main characters are...
The problem in the story is...
The twist comes when...
The moral of the story is...
0 words
Use the sentence frame above. Start with what the story IS (a modern retelling). Then say who the characters are and what they want. What is the problem? What is the twist? End with the moral. Don't include details like the exact words they said — just the main events!
📋 Model Answer (92 words)
Auntie Anthea and Gentle Geoffrey is a modern retelling of the fable The Ant and the Grasshopper. The story is set in a meadow during the changing seasons. The two main characters are Anthea, a hardworking ant, and Geoffrey, a musical grasshopper. The problem is that Geoffrey has spent all summer playing music and has no food or shelter for winter. The twist comes when Anthea, who plans to eat Geoffrey, is so charmed by his music that she forgets her scheme. They make a deal, and both benefit. The moral is: one good turn deserves another.
Key features: covers all 6 frame points, no unnecessary detail, clear sequence, under 100 words.

💬 Discussion Questions

Think carefully and write detailed answers. There is no single right answer — use evidence!

1 Do you think Anthea was really going to eat Geoffrey? Find evidence from the story to support your answer.
Look for the clues in what Anthea SAYS. What words does she use that suggest she wants to eat him? Find at least TWO pieces of evidence (quotes).
📋 Suggested Answer
Yes, I think Anthea was really planning to eat Geoffrey. There is strong evidence: she says "What a tasty treat!" and "Geoffrey will make a delicious difference!" before he even arrives. Then she accidentally says "delicious, I mean delightful" — this slip reveals her true intentions. Her comment "A bit of a bite, of course!" is another clue. All of these suggest she had a very specific plan for Geoffrey's fate — as food, not as a tenant!
2 Geoffrey says Anthea seems "so kind" — but was she really being kind? Explain your answer.
Think about dramatic irony — Geoffrey THINKS she is kind, but what does the reader know that Geoffrey doesn't? What is Anthea's real motive for inviting him in?
📋 Suggested Answer
Anthea was NOT genuinely being kind. Her real motive was selfish — she wanted to eat Geoffrey, not help him. Geoffrey believes she is kind because he doesn't understand her hidden clues. This is an example of dramatic irony: the reader knows her true plan, but Geoffrey does not. By the END, Anthea does show genuine kindness by agreeing to give Geoffrey shelter in exchange for music — but her original intention was entirely self-serving.
3 Which ending do you think is fairer — the original (Grasshopper suffers) or the modern (Geoffrey survives)? Why?
Think about: Does Grasshopper/Geoffrey deserve a second chance? Does suffering teach a better lesson than being helped? What does each ending say about how we should treat others?
📋 Suggested Answer
I think the modern ending is fairer because it gives Geoffrey a chance to use his talent (music) to help himself, rather than simply suffering for his mistake. It also rewards Anthea for being open-minded enough to listen. The original ending is very harsh — Grasshopper gets no help at all, which could seem cruel rather than fair. The modern moral ("one good turn deserves another") feels more realistic: people can change and help each other rather than just being punished.
Accept either answer — mark for clear reasoning with reference to both versions.
4 What does "One good turn deserves another" mean? How does this apply to Geoffrey and Anthea at the end of the story?
First explain the proverb. Then think: who does a good turn first? What do they receive in return? How does their "deal" match the meaning of the proverb?
📋 Suggested Answer
"One good turn deserves another" means that if you do something kind or helpful for someone, they should do something kind in return. In the story, Anthea gives Geoffrey warmth and shelter (her good turn), and in return Geoffrey plays beautiful music to keep her company through the long winter (his good turn). Each character benefits from helping the other — their deal perfectly illustrates the proverb.

🧠 Comprehension

Exam-style questions — write full, detailed answers.

2 marks
Q1. Why does Anthea say "delicious, I mean delightful"? What does this tell us about her plan?
Think about: what is a "slip of the tongue"? What does it reveal? What is "dramatic irony"? How does the reader feel compared to Geoffrey?
📋 Model Answer (2 marks)
Anthea accidentally says "delicious" when she means "delightful" — this is a slip of the tongue that reveals her true plan: she intends to eat Geoffrey, not simply invite him as a friendly tenant. The writer uses this slip to create humour and dramatic irony — the reader knows Anthea's real motive before Geoffrey does.
Mark scheme: 1 mark for explaining the slip reveals her plan to eat Geoffrey. 1 mark for identifying the effect — humour/dramatic irony and that the reader knows more than Geoffrey.
3 marks
Q2. How is the modern version different from the original fable? Give THREE differences and explain why the writer may have made these changes.
Use the comparison table on the Compare tab. Choose THREE clear differences. For each one, explain WHY the writer might have made this change — think about: what effect does this create? What new message does it give?
📋 Model Answer (3 marks)
First, the modern version has dark humour — Anthea's plan to eat Geoffrey is darkly funny, unlike the original's straightforward moral lesson. Second, Geoffrey survives and even benefits, whereas the original Grasshopper is left to suffer with no resolution. Third, the modern moral ('one good turn deserves another') is more positive — it rewards cleverness rather than just punishing laziness. The writer may have made these changes to create a more entertaining, modern story that shows both characters benefiting, making the lesson feel more hopeful.
Mark scheme: 1 mark per difference correctly identified with explanation of the writer's purpose (up to 3 marks).
3 marks
Q3. What literary technique does the writer use when Anthea says "delicious, I mean delightful"? Explain the effect this creates for the reader.
Name the technique (dramatic irony). Then explain: what does the reader know? What does Geoffrey NOT know? What two feelings does this create in the reader?
📋 Model Answer (3 marks)
The writer uses dramatic irony — the reader understands something that one character (Geoffrey) does not. When Anthea corrects herself from 'delicious' to 'delightful', the reader immediately understands her real plan, but Geoffrey remains confused. This creates both humour and tension: we are amused by Anthea's slip, but we also feel worried for Geoffrey, who is unknowingly in danger. It makes us want to read on to find out what happens.
Mark scheme: 1 mark — names dramatic irony. 1 mark — explains the reader knows more than Geoffrey. 1 mark — identifies two effects (humour AND tension/suspense) with explanation.
