📖 Narrative Voice

Who is telling the story? That's the narrative voice!

Two Main Narrative Voices

1st Person Narrator — the storyteller IS a character in the story. They use I, me, we, us, my, our.
Example: "I ran as fast as I could."


3rd Person Narrator — the storyteller is OUTSIDE the story. They use he, she, they, it, his, her, their.
Example: "She ran as fast as she could."

The Ant & the Grasshopper — Which Voice?

The story uses a 3rd person narrator for the storytelling — the narrator is NOT a character. But the characters speak in 1st person when they talk!

Part of storyVoiceExample
Narration (story)3rd Person"A grasshopper was bouncing about..."
Narration (story)3rd Person"She continued on her industrious way."
Character dialogue1st Person"Come and chat with me," said Grasshopper.
Character dialogue1st Person"I am too busy," said Ant.

💡 Key Rule

Narrative voice = the voice TELLING the story, NOT the dialogue inside it. Characters always speak in 1st person even in a 3rd person story!

🔑 Personal Pronouns

Pronouns replace nouns so we don't repeat the same word.

Subject vs Object Pronouns

Subject pronoun — the pronoun IS the one doing the action.
Example: "She carried the corn." — She is doing the carrying.


Object pronoun — the pronoun receives the action, or comes after a verb/preposition.
Example: "Ant ignored him." — Him is being ignored.

💡 Quick Test

To check: Can you use it at the START of a sentence doing something? → Subject pronoun.
Does it come AFTER a verb? → Object pronoun.
"She saw him." — She = subject, him = object.

💎 Possessives

Two types — possessive adjectives and possessive pronouns!

What's the difference?

Possessive Adjective — comes BEFORE a noun. It describes who owns something.
Example: "That is my book." — "my" comes before "book".


Possessive Pronoun — stands ALONE. It replaces the noun.
Example: "That book is mine." — "mine" replaces "my book".

⚠️ Common Mistake: its vs it's

its (no apostrophe) = possessive adjective. "The ant carried its food."
it's (with apostrophe) = it IS. "It's a cold winter." = "It is a cold winter."
Never write "it's" to mean possession!

✏️ Retell from Grasshopper's Point of View

Retell the opening of the fable as GRASSHOPPER using 1st person (I, me, my).

Your Task

The original story is told by a 3rd person narrator. Retell the OPENING from Grasshopper's perspective — use "I" as if you ARE the grasshopper. Use the sentence starters and word bank below!

📚 Word Bank — click to copy a word:
chirruping fathom plentiful foolish wistfully

Sentence Starters — use these to help you!

1. "One fine summer's day, I was bouncing about..."
2. "When I saw Ant lugging that huge ear of corn, I couldn't fathom why she was..."
3. "I teased her, saying '...'"
4. "Looking back now, I realise I was..."

Remember: change "the grasshopper" → "I", "he" → "I", "his" → "my". Keep the STORY events the same, just change who is speaking them. You can show Grasshopper's carefree, happy feelings — he doesn't know he's doing anything wrong yet!
📋 Model Answer
One fine summer's day, I was bouncing about, chirruping merrily in the warm sunshine. Food was plentiful everywhere and I felt so alive! Then I spotted Ant — she was lugging an enormous ear of corn on her back. I couldn't fathom why she would work so hard when summer was so glorious. "Why not come and chat with me?" I teased her. "Winter is far, far away!" She shook her head and carried on her industrious way. I just laughed and played on. Looking back now, I realise I was foolish. I watched her wistfully as the cold crept in.
✅ Notice: she/her are still used for Ant, but I/my/me for Grasshopper. Correct 1st person throughout!

🧠 Comprehension

Exam-style questions — write full, detailed answers.

2 marks
Q1. Who narrates The Ant and the Grasshopper? How do you know? Use evidence from the text.
Think: does the narrator say "I" in the storytelling parts? What pronouns does the narrator use to describe Ant and Grasshopper? Find a quote as evidence.
📋 Model Answer (2 marks)
The story is narrated by a third-person narrator — someone outside the story who is not a character. We know this because the narrator uses third-person pronouns: "a grasshopper was bouncing about" and "she continued on her industrious way". The narrator never says "I" in the storytelling sections.
Mark scheme: 1 mark for identifying 3rd person narrator. 1 mark for evidence/example from the text.
2 marks
Q2. Choose the correct word: "Ant said the food was (her / hers) to keep." Explain why the other option is wrong.
Ask yourself: is there a NOUN immediately after the blank? Possessive adjectives come before a noun. Possessive pronouns stand alone.
📋 Model Answer (2 marks)
The correct word is hers: "Ant said the food was hers to keep." We use the possessive pronoun "hers" here because it stands alone — there is no noun directly after it. "Her" is a possessive adjective and must come before a noun (e.g., "her food"), not at the end of a sentence.
Mark scheme: 1 mark for choosing "hers". 1 mark for explaining the rule correctly.
3 marks
Q3. Rewrite this paragraph changing it from 3rd to 1st person (Ant speaking):
"She scurried back and forth, gathering food for her nest. She never stopped to admire the glorious day."
Begin: "I scurried..."
Change: she → I, her → my. Keep everything else EXACTLY the same — don't change the meaning or add new ideas. Two sentences needed.
📋 Model Answer (3 marks)
I scurried back and forth, gathering food for my nest. I never stopped to admire the glorious day.
Mark scheme: 1 mark — she→I correct. 1 mark — her→my correct. 1 mark — no other changes to meaning, grammatically correct.
