🎭 Types of Figurative Language
Figurative language makes writing more interesting and exciting. Learn the 4 main types!

What is figurative language?

Figurative language uses words in a creative, non-literal way to create pictures in the reader's mind. It makes writing more vivid, memorable and powerful.

SIMILE
Comparing two things using "like" or "as". A simile says something is LIKE something else.
🔹 "The ant worked AS HARD AS a machine."
🔹 "Grasshopper sang LIKE a bird without a care."
🔹 "She ran as fast as the wind."
✏️ Try your own simile:
METAPHOR
Saying something IS something else — without using like or as. It makes a direct comparison.
🔹 "Winter was a cruel thief, stealing all joy."
🔹 "The meadow was a golden carpet in summer."
🔹 "The ant was a tiny machine that never stopped."
✏️ Try your own metaphor:
PERSONIFICATION
Giving human qualities to non-human things (animals, objects, weather, ideas). It brings things to life!
🔹 "Winter came, bringing barren fare and frosty fields." (winter chooses to come, like a person)
🔹 "The sun smiled down on the meadow."
🔹 "The trees whispered in the breeze."
✏️ Try your own personification:
ALLITERATION
Using the same consonant sound at the start of several nearby words. It makes writing musical and memorable!
🔹 "Auntie Anthea" — A sound repeated
🔹 "Gentle Geoffrey" — G sound repeated
🔹 "Singing, scurrying, storing" — S sound repeated
🔹 "Singing and jigging" — soft sounds

Why do writers use alliteration?

  • 🎵 It makes writing sound musical and pleasant to read aloud
  • 🧠 It makes characters and phrases memorable and easier to remember
  • 😄 It can be fun and playful, especially in names
  • ✨ It adds rhythm and energy to descriptions

Examples from the stories

Auntie Anthea — both names start with the same A sound
Gentle Geoffrey — G sound repeated to match his gentle personality
Singing, scurrying, storing — three S words in a row create a busy, buzzing rhythm
Singing and jigging — soft sounds make the scene feel light and carefree

What is an idiom?

An idiom is a phrase where the words together have a DIFFERENT meaning from what they say literally. For example, "break a leg" does NOT mean actually break your leg — it means good luck!

🧠 Comprehension Practice
Exam-style questions on figurative language. Read carefully and show what you know!
1 mark
Q1. What is the difference between a simile and a metaphor? Give one example of each.
Think about the key words: a simile uses "like" or "as". What words does a metaphor NOT use?
⭐ Model Answer (1 mark)
A simile compares two things using 'like' or 'as': "She worked as hard as a machine."
A metaphor says something IS something else (no like/as): "Life is a rollercoaster."
3 marks
Q2. Find THREE examples of figurative language in this passage and identify the technique used in each. Explain the effect of ONE of them.
"Summer was a golden dream, warm and endless. Ant scurried back and forth like a tiny engine that never switched off. When winter finally came knocking at the door, Grasshopper's joy melted like snow in a warm hand."
Look for: a phrase that uses "like" (simile), a phrase that says something IS something (metaphor), a non-human thing doing a human action (personification).
⭐ Model Answer (3 marks)
1. "Summer was a golden dream" = metaphor — it compares summer to a dream, suggesting it felt unreal and perfect.

2. "like a tiny engine that never switched off" = simile — compares Ant to an engine, emphasising her non-stop, mechanical work.

3. "winter finally came knocking at the door" = personification — winter is given the human action of knocking, making it seem like an inevitable, unwelcome visitor.

Effect of the metaphor: Calling summer "a golden dream" makes it sound magical and perfect — which makes the arrival of winter feel like an even harsher awakening.
3 marks
Q3. Write a short paragraph (4–6 sentences) describing a character or scene from the fable using AT LEAST TWO different types of figurative language. Label them.
Character/scene to describe: Choose one — Ant working in summer / Grasshopper in winter / the meadow in summer / the first snowfall

Plan:
Simile: _______ like / as _______
Metaphor or Personification: _______
Try describing Grasshopper in winter. Simile: "He shivered like..." Metaphor: "The cold was a..." Don't forget to label them!
⭐ Model Answer (3 marks)
The meadow in winter was a frozen desert, silent and empty [METAPHOR]. Snow had crept across the ground like a cold white blanket, smothering every blade of grass [SIMILE + PERSONIFICATION]. Grasshopper stood at Ant's door, trembling like a leaf in the wind [SIMILE]. The cold air bit at his wings and clawed at his face [PERSONIFICATION]. He was no longer the carefree musician of summer — he was a broken instrument, silent at last [METAPHOR].

Labels used: metaphor × 2, simile × 2, personification × 2 — full marks!
