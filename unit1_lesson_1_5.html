🌟 What is a Proverb?

A proverb is a short, wise saying that gives advice or states a truth about life.

Definition

A proverb is a short, well-known saying that gives advice or states a truth about life. Think of it like a fable — but squeezed into ONE sentence!

Proverbs have:
✦ A literal meaning (what the words actually say)
✦ A figurative/deeper meaning (the real lesson or advice)

🔗 Proverbs and Fables

Many fables END with a proverb — the moral of the story! The Ant and the Grasshopper's moral is basically the proverb: "Make hay while the sun shines." The grasshopper didn't, so he suffered!

🎭 Literal vs Figurative Language

Does the language mean EXACTLY what it says, or something different?

LITERAL language

Means exactly what it says — no hidden meaning. The words are used in their normal, dictionary sense.
Example: "The ant carried a grain of corn." — She really did this!

FIGURATIVE language

Means something DIFFERENT from what the words literally say — there is a deeper or hidden meaning.
Example: "The grasshopper had his head in the clouds." — His head wasn't actually in the clouds! It means he was daydreaming and not being realistic.

✏️ Apostrophes

Apostrophes do TWO jobs — possession and contraction.

RULE 1 — Possession (belonging)

Add 's to show something belongs to ONE person or thing.
the ant's food = the food belonging to ONE ant

For plural nouns already ending in s: add just ' (apostrophe only).
the ants' colony = the colony belonging to MANY ants

RULE 2 — Contraction (missing letters)

An apostrophe shows where letters have been removed when two words are joined.
will + not = won't
did + not = didn't
it + is = it's
summer + is = summer's day (in the phrase "summer's day" = the day OF summer)

⚠️ Special Case: its vs it's

its (NO apostrophe) = possessive. "The ant carried its food." (the food belonging to IT)
it's (WITH apostrophe) = it IS. "It's cold outside." = "It is cold outside."
Test: Replace with "it is" — does it make sense? If yes → it's. If no → its.

🧠 Comprehension + Cartoon Plan

Exam-style questions and creative planning.

2 marks
Q1. What does the proverb "A friend in need is a friend indeed" mean? How could this proverb apply to the fable of Ant and Grasshopper?
First explain what the proverb means (help in hard times). Then think about whether Ant and Grasshopper's relationship matches this — did Ant help Grasshopper when he was in need?
📋 Model Answer (2 marks)
This proverb means that a true friend is someone who helps you when you are in trouble, not just when things are easy. In the fable, it could apply if Ant had chosen to help Grasshopper in winter — she would then have proved herself a true friend. However, Ant did NOT help Grasshopper, which shows their relationship was not one of close friendship.
Mark scheme: 1 mark for explaining the proverb's meaning. 1 mark for connecting it to the fable with detail.
2 marks
Q2. Explain the difference between literal and figurative language. Give one example of each from The Ant and the Grasshopper.
Define BOTH terms first. Then give a quote for each type — make sure your examples are clearly from the fable. Check: does the literal example describe something that REALLY happened? Does the figurative example have a hidden meaning?
📋 Model Answer (2 marks)
Literal language means exactly what it says — for example, "the ant scurried back and forth gathering food" is literal because the ant really did this. Figurative language means something different from what the words literally say — for example, "Grasshopper's song stuck in his throat" is figurative because a song cannot physically stick; it means he was too miserable to sing.
Mark scheme: 1 mark for defining both terms. 1 mark for a correct example of each with explanation.
