🔑 What Are Modal Verbs?
Modal verbs are helper verbs that change how certain or necessary something is.

📌 The Simple Explanation

A modal verb changes another verb to show:
• How certain something is (might it happen? will it definitely happen?)
• How necessary something is (should you do it? must you do it?)
• Ability (can you do it?)
• Permission (may you do it?)

Key rule: Modal verbs are ALWAYS followed by the base verb (the simplest form). ✅ "She should go" — NOT "She should goes" ❌

📊 The Certainty Scale — How sure are we?
Not sure at all ←————————————————————→ Absolutely certain
mightcouldmayshouldwillmust

📚 Examples from the Story

• "Summer won't last forever" — Ant uses WILL (certain future). She knows winter is definitely coming.
• "You should be doing the same" — Ant uses SHOULD (obligation/advice). She thinks it's important for Grasshopper to prepare.
• "The grasshopper might collect food" — MIGHT (possible but not sure — and as we know, he won't!)
• "I can dream" — CAN (ability). Shows what someone is able to do.

✅ Quick Check — Which modal fits best?

Obligation (you have to): must, should, ought to
Possibility (maybe): might, may, could
Ability (able to do): can, could
Certainty (definitely will): will, shall, must

⚠️ Exam Tip: Negative Modal Verbs

Add NOT (or a contraction) to make a modal negative:
• will → will not / won't
• should → should not / shouldn't
• can → cannot / can't
• must → must not / mustn't
• might → might not (no common contraction)

📌 Why does this matter for your writing?

Using the right verb makes your writing much more vivid and precise. Instead of always writing "said", a good writer uses words like "snapped", "whispered", "declared" — each one gives the reader a different picture!

✏️ Comprehension Questions
These questions ask you HOW the writer creates effects. Use PEE in every answer!

🔍 "How does the writer show...?" — How to answer

Step 1 — Identify WHAT the writer is trying to show (e.g. Ant's annoyance).
Step 2 — Find a SPECIFIC WORD or phrase from the text as evidence.
Step 3 — Explain WHY that word/phrase shows the effect.

Example: "The writer shows Ant is angry through the word 'snapped'. This suggests she was sharp and annoyed, like a dog snapping its teeth."

Question 1 2 marks
Why did Ant say "Summer won't last forever"? What was she trying to do by saying this? Use evidence from the story.
💡 Think about: what does the word "won't" tell us? What was Ant's purpose — to warn Grasshopper, threaten him, or help him?
The modal verb "won't" (will not) shows certainty — Ant KNOWS winter is coming. Why would she tell this to Grasshopper? Look at what she says right after: "you should be doing the same".
✅ Model Answer (2 marks)
Ant said "Summer won't last forever" to warn Grasshopper to prepare for winter. The modal verb "won't" shows Ant is completely certain that winter will come — she is not guessing. She follows this with "you should be doing the same", showing that her purpose was to advise Grasshopper to collect food while he still could.
📝 Key skill: identifying a modal verb AND explaining the effect it creates.
Question 2 3 marks
"Grasshopper's song stuck in his throat." What does this expression mean literally and figuratively? What does it tell us about how Grasshopper felt?
💡 Literally = exactly what the words say. Figuratively = what it really means in the story. Think about WHY his song would "stick".
Literally: Can a song really "stick" in a throat? What would that mean physically? Figuratively: If someone is very shocked or miserable, what happens to their voice? Think about a time you were so upset you couldn't speak.
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
Literally, this expression means that a piece of a song became stuck inside Grasshopper's throat like food getting stuck when you swallow — but this is impossible, so it is figurative. Figuratively, it means that Grasshopper was so miserable and shocked by the cold and hunger that he could no longer sing. He had lost his only talent and joy. This tells us that Grasshopper felt deeply ashamed and hopeless — a complete contrast to his carefree, singing self in summer.
📝 Marks: 1 for literal meaning, 1 for figurative meaning, 1 for explaining Grasshopper's feelings with reference to contrast (summer vs winter).
Question 3 3 marks
Look at the verbs used to describe how Ant moves: "bustled", "scurry", "toiling". What do these verbs tell us about Ant's character? Explain the effect of each.
💡 For each verb: (1) What does it mean exactly? (2) What image does it create? (3) What does it tell us about Ant?
"Bustled" — think of a busy person rushing about. "Scurry" — this is often used for small, fast animals (mice scurry). "Toiling" — this comes from "toil" meaning hard, exhausting work. Together, what picture of Ant do these verbs create?
✅ Model Answer (3 marks)
"Bustled" suggests Ant moved in a busy, energetic way — always in motion, never resting. "Scurry" creates an image of rapid, purposeful movement back and forth, like a small creature with urgent tasks to complete. "Toiling" suggests hard, exhausting physical work — it is a more serious word than simply "working", suggesting Ant pushed herself to her limits. Together, these three verbs portray Ant as determined, tireless, and completely focused on her goal — the opposite of Grasshopper, who simply hops and sings.
📝 1 mark per verb with a clear explanation of the effect on meaning/character.
Question 4 4 marks
What tense is the narrative (storytelling) part of the fable written in? How does this affect the way the story feels? Contrast this with the dialogue (speech). Give examples of each from the text.
💡 Tense = past or present? Look at the verbs: "was", "bustled", "came" — are these past or present? Now look at dialogue: "Take it easy!" "Summer won't last!" — are these different?
Narrative verbs: "was bouncing", "bustled by", "came" — past tense (it already happened). Dialogue verbs: "Take it easy", "Why are you working", "Summer won't last" — present/future tense (happening NOW). The past tense makes a story feel like it already happened. The present tense dialogue makes it feel alive and immediate.
✅ Model Answer (4 marks)
The narrative part of the fable is written in the past tense. For example: "a grasshopper was bouncing about", "Winter came, bringing barren fare". This makes the story feel like a finished event — something that has already happened, which is appropriate for a traditional fable being retold. However, the dialogue is mainly written in the present tense. For example: "Why are you working so hard?" and "Summer won't last forever." Using present tense in dialogue makes the characters feel alive and immediate — as if the conversation is happening right now in front of us. This contrast between past narrative and present dialogue is a common storytelling technique.
📝 Marks: 1 for identifying past tense + example. 1 for identifying present tense in dialogue + example. 1 for explaining the effect of past tense. 1 for explaining the effect of present tense in dialogue.
